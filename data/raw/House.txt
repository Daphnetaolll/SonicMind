Title: House Music

House music is a style of electronic dance music that emerged in Chicago in the early to mid-1980s. It grew from disco, soul, funk, and synth-based club music, then developed its own identity through drum machines, looping grooves, basslines, and DJ-friendly arrangements. The name "house" is commonly linked to the Warehouse club and the scene around DJ Frankie Knuckles.

A typical house track uses a steady 4/4 pulse with a kick drum on every beat, which creates the familiar four-on-the-floor feel. Tempos often sit around 120 to 130 BPM, although the exact range varies by subgenre. Producers often combine drum machine patterns with basslines, piano riffs, vocal hooks, sampled textures, and repeated transitions that work well in a club mix.

House became one of the foundational branches of modern dance music because it proved both flexible and widely accessible. Over time it expanded into many subgenres, including deep house, progressive house, acid house, tropical house, and tech house. Some versions emphasize soulful vocals and warmth, while others focus on groove, texture, or stripped-back repetition.

When people describe house music, they usually point to three qualities: a danceable and stable rhythm, a warm groove rooted in disco and funk traditions, and a structure designed for mixing and gradual movement on a dance floor. Because of that balance between repetition and musicality, house remains one of the most approachable entry points into electronic music.
